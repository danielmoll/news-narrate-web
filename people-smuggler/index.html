People Smuggler:World’s Most Wanted

Almost 150,000 migrants have crossed the Mediterranean into Europe in the first six months of 2015. The 180-mile journey from North Africa to one of Europe's southernmost points claims thousands of lives every year, but for the migrants braving the Mediterranean, it is the final leg of a long, dangerous and expensive journey. Take a look at that journey by following this interactive route from the centre of Sudan to one of the many entrances to Europe dotted along the region's southern coast.

Khartoum, Sudan

The start of many migrants’ journeys to Europe. A bustling metropolitan area of over five million people that is a magnet for refugees fleeing conflict in neighbouring countries. Many migrants meet their handlers at the Omdurman Market and negotiate a price for the first stage of their journey north. But for many, they'll never reach Europe.

Kufra, Libya

876 miles

Kufra was for centuries a staging post on slave routes. Today it is the entry point for all sorts of contraband into Libya. Locals have tried to prevent illicit trade into the town with The Great Wall Of Kufra, which stretches 150km around the town. It struggles to stop the flow of people north with the harsh reality of Saharan sand and the well-equipped smugglers easily rendering it useless.

How is an ancient stop on Africa's trading routes now under the control of people smugglers?
These women have paid thousands of dollars to cross the Sahara, but are now stuck in Kufra.

Smugglers store supplies and weapons at hidden camps in the desert. The Libyan authorities will not take them on. Migrants say the Sahara Desert is like another sea they have to cross if they have any chance of reaching Europe.

Benghazi, Libya

1495 miles

Libya's second city is the frontline of the country's civil war. Fighters from the National Army and the internationally-recognised Tobruk government clash with Islamic State fighters trying to spread the group's poisonous message across the Middle East. The dangerous conditions don't hinder the smugglers from moving men, women and children through the city.

Having been herded across deserts like human cattle, migrants now face being caught up in a civil war.
The threat from the Islamic State is clear in Benghazi.

The fighting provides the perfect anarchic conditions for people-smuggling gangs to thrive.

Tripoli, Libya

2126 miles

Tripoli seems like any other city in the Middle East - a thriving, bustling home to millions of people. But it is a facade. A government unrecognised by the outside world is in charge and smugglers have a free rein in large areas of the city. They use dilapidated houses and old factories to store their cargo until passage can be arranged. The wait for the right sea conditions can take weeks. Sometimes though, the migrants who have travelled over 2,000 miles to get here are simply held until more money is wired to their smugglers.

Tripoli may look safe, but hidden below its rooftops is a city in the grip of dangerous smugglers.
People are held for months in tiny prisons, and they aren't happy to see their guards.

Zuwara, Libya

2199 miles

It should only take a few hours to get to Europe from Zuwara. A good boat could make the journey in a single sunny Mediterranean afternoon. But the migrants desperate to cross to southern Europe face an arduous trip in rickety old boats that the smugglers can afford to lose. They are packed on board, with those who pay the least being locked in the hold. Thousands leave Zuwara for Europe, but hundreds never get there.

It may be closer than ever but there is still one deadly step on the journey to Europe.
Migrants are kept in back-street safe houses before handing over their final dollars to smugglers who send them to Europe from a local beach.

Hundreds never make it - bodies wash up on the shore here, and the local cemetery is packed.

Lampedusa, Italy

2379 miles

For the migrants who make it to Lampedusa, the journey is simply half-done. They may encounter some smiling faces for the first time in weeks as medics and aid workers help them from the boat, but they now have to reach mainland Europe. They are exhausted and terrified, but for many the dream of a life in the comfort of northern Europe's major cities will spur them on to face another dangerous journey.

Setting foot on European soil may feel like the end of their journey, but smuggled migrants rapidly discover they are simply at the start of another difficult battle to get into Europe.
For those tasked with helping forlorn migrants arriving in Europe, the reality of people smuggling can be tough to cope with.
